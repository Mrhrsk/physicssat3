Unit A — Charged Particles

Quantisation of charge, deflection in fields, particle classification

12.2.1.2

Quantisation of Charge — Millikan's Experiment

The idea

Electric charge comes in discrete packets. You can never find a charge of 0.5e or 2.7e — it's always an integer multiple of the elementary charge e = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.

Millikan's oil drop experiment

Tiny charged oil drops are suspended between two parallel plates. By adjusting the voltage until the electric force balances gravity, you can calculate the charge on each drop. The key findings:

  • All measured charges are integer multiples of a smallest value
  • That smallest value is e ≈ 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
  • This proves charge is quantised (discrete, not continuous)
Exam approach: Given a set of charge values on oil drops (like 3.2, 6.4, 9.7, 16, 12.8, 3.1, 6.3 × 10⁻¹⁹ C), find the greatest common divisor. All values should be approximately integer multiples of the fundamental charge. Here: all are roughly multiples of 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (which is 2e).

When the drop is stationary (in equilibrium):

Electric force = Weight qE = mg q(V/d) = mg q = mgd / V

where V = voltage between plates, d = plate separation, m = mass of drop, g = gravitational acceleration.

12.2.1.3

Deflection of Charged Particles in E & B Fields

In an electric field (between parallel plates)

  • Positive charges deflect towards the negative plate
  • Negative charges deflect towards the positive plate
  • Uncharged particles (γ-rays) are not deflected
  • The deflection depends on charge/mass ratio — lighter particles (β) deflect much more than heavier ones (α)

In a magnetic field

  • Force is perpendicular to both velocity and B-field: F = qvB (when v ⊥ B)
  • This causes circular motion, not straight-line deflection
  • Positive and negative charges curve in opposite directions (use Fleming's left-hand rule)
  • γ-rays are unaffected
  • α-particles curve less than β-particles (greater mass → greater radius of curvature: r = mv/qB)
Fleming's Left-Hand Rule: Thumb = Force (motion), First finger = Field (B), Second finger = Current (conventional, +ve charge direction). For negative charges, reverse the force direction.

Velocity Selector

A velocity selector uses crossed E and B fields (perpendicular to each other and to the beam). Only particles with a specific velocity pass through undeflected:

Electric force = Magnetic force qE = qvB v = E / B

For positive ions moving to the right: if E points up, B must point out of the page (or into, depending on geometry) — so that the electric force (upward) is balanced by the magnetic force (downward). The key is that qE must equal qvB in opposing directions.

Exam trap (Q6 type): For positive ions moving right, the E and B fields must produce forces in opposite directions on the ion. Option C in the spec has E upward and B out of the page — this gives electric force up and magnetic force down (by F = qv×B), balancing them.
12.2.1.5

Determining v and e/mₑ for Electrons

Cathode Ray Tube (CRT) Method

This is the standard method to determine both velocity and the specific charge (e/mₑ) of electrons:

  1. Electrons are emitted from a heated cathode (thermionic emission)
  2. They're accelerated through a known potential difference V
  3. The kinetic energy gained equals the electrical energy: eV = ½mv²
  4. From this: v = √(2eV/m)

Finding e/mₑ

Once the electrons enter a magnetic field B, they follow a circular path of radius r:

qvB = mv²/r (magnetic force = centripetal force) e/mₑ = v/(Br) Combined with eV = ½mv²: e/mₑ = 2V/(B²r²)
Key idea: The CRT method uses an electric field to accelerate electrons to known energy, then a magnetic field to bend them. By measuring the radius of curvature and knowing V and B, you get e/mₑ ≈ 1.76 × 10¹¹ C/kg.

Unit B — Quantum Physics

Photons, photoelectric effect, de Broglie, energy levels, spectra

12.2.2.2

Photon Energy: E = hf

Electromagnetic radiation is not a smooth wave — it comes in discrete packets called photons. Each photon carries an energy that depends on its frequency:

E = hf Also, since c = fλ: E = hc / λ

where h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s (Planck constant), f = frequency (Hz), λ = wavelength (m).

The electronvolt (eV)

Photon energies are tiny in joules, so we often use electronvolts:

1 eV = 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ J To convert J → eV: divide by 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ To convert eV → J: multiply by 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹
Worked example: Energy of a photon with f = 5.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz

E = hf = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ × 5.0 × 10¹⁴ = 3.315 × 10⁻¹⁹ J ≈ 3.3 × 10⁻¹⁹ J

Higher frequency = higher energy. Gamma photons have the most energy; radio photons the least. Energy is inversely proportional to wavelength.
12.2.2.6

The Photoelectric Effect

What happens

When electromagnetic radiation (usually UV) shines on a metal surface, electrons can be emitted from the surface. These are called photoelectrons.

Key observations — evidence for particle nature of light

  1. Emission is instantaneous — no time delay between illumination and electron emission
  2. There exists a threshold frequency f₀ — below this frequency, NO electrons are emitted regardless of intensity
  3. Increasing intensity increases the number of photoelectrons (current), NOT their kinetic energy
  4. Increasing frequency (above threshold) increases the maximum kinetic energy of electrons
  5. One photon interacts with one electron (one-to-one interaction)

Why wave theory fails

Classical wave theory predicts that any frequency should work if the light is intense enough (more energy delivered by brighter light), and that there should be a delay as electrons "collect" energy. Both predictions are wrong. The existence of a threshold frequency and instantaneous emission can only be explained by treating light as photons.

Work function (Φ)

The work function is the minimum energy needed to remove an electron from the metal surface. It depends only on the type of metal, NOT on the light.

At the threshold frequency: hf₀ = Φ (all photon energy goes to just freeing the electron) f₀ = Φ / h
12.2.2.7

Work Function vs Intensity

Why is the work function independent of intensity?

The work function is a property of the metal — it's the energy binding the most loosely held surface electrons. It has nothing to do with the light hitting it.

Light intensity = number of photons per second. Increasing intensity means MORE photons arrive, but each photon still has the same energy (hf). If hf < Φ, no single photon has enough energy to free an electron, no matter how many arrive.

Why is photoelectric current proportional to intensity?

If f ≥ f₀ (photon energy ≥ work function), then each photon can release one electron. More photons per second (higher intensity) → more electrons released per second → more current.

Summary: Work function depends on the energy per photon (frequency). Photoelectric current depends on the number of photons (intensity). These are independent quantities.
12.2.2.8

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

hf = Φ + ½mv²ₘₐₓ Energy of photon = Work function + Max kinetic energy of electron

This comes directly from conservation of energy. The photon gives ALL its energy to one electron. Some energy (Φ) is used to escape the metal; the rest becomes kinetic energy.

Understanding the equation

  • If hf = Φ: the electron just barely escapes with zero KE (this is the threshold case)
  • If hf > Φ: excess energy appears as kinetic energy of the electron
  • If hf < Φ: the electron CANNOT escape — no photoelectric effect

The graph of Eₖ(max) vs frequency

The graph is a straight line: Eₖ = hf − Φ

  • Gradient = h (Planck constant)
  • x-intercept = f₀ (threshold frequency)
  • y-intercept = −Φ (negative work function)
  • For a different metal with lower work function: the line shifts left (lower threshold frequency) but has the same gradient (h is universal)
Exam tip for Q20 type: From the graph, read the threshold frequency where the line crosses the x-axis. To find h, pick two points on the line and calculate gradient = ΔEₖ/Δf. For a lower work function metal, draw a parallel line to the left.

Why electrons have a RANGE of kinetic energies

The maximum KE corresponds to electrons at the surface. Electrons from deeper inside the metal need extra energy to reach the surface first, so they emerge with less KE. This is why the equation gives the maximum KE.

Worked example: Q18 type — does a second lamp affect the photoelectric current?

Given: zinc work function Φ = 5.8 × 10⁻¹⁹ J. UV lamp at λ = 120 nm, second lamp at λ = 450 nm.

Threshold wavelength: λ₀ = hc/Φ = (6.63×10⁻³⁴ × 3×10⁸) / 5.8×10⁻¹⁹ = 343 nm

The UV (120 nm < 343 nm) → above threshold → causes photoelectric effect ✓

The visible light (450 nm > 343 nm) → below threshold → cannot cause any photoelectric emission

Therefore: switching on the second lamp has no effect on the photoelectric current.

12.2.2.10

de Broglie Wavelength

All matter has wave-like properties. The wavelength associated with a moving particle is:

λ = h / p = h / (mv) λ = de Broglie wavelength (m) h = Planck constant p = momentum (kg·m/s) m = mass, v = velocity

This was confirmed by electron diffraction — electrons fired through thin graphite produce ring patterns, just like wave diffraction. This only works because their wavelength (~10⁻¹⁰ m) is comparable to atomic spacing.

Wave–particle duality

  • Light travels as a wave (diffraction, interference) but interacts as particles (photoelectric effect)
  • Electrons travel as waves (diffraction) but interact as particles (Newtonian mechanics)
Worked example: Q16 — de Broglie wavelength of an electron at 2.0 × 10⁶ m/s

λ = h/(mv) = 6.63×10⁻³⁴ / (9.1×10⁻³¹ × 2.0×10⁶)

λ = 6.63×10⁻³⁴ / 1.82×10⁻²⁴ = 3.6 × 10⁻¹⁰ m

(Note: the spec has a typo "9.1×10⁻³⁴" in the mark scheme — it should be 10⁻³¹)

12.2.2.12

Emission & Absorption Line Spectra

Emission line spectrum

A hot gas at low pressure produces bright coloured lines on a dark background. Each line corresponds to a specific wavelength emitted when electrons in atoms drop from higher energy levels to lower ones, releasing photons.

Absorption line spectrum

When white light passes through a cool gas, certain wavelengths are missing (dark lines on a continuous rainbow background). The cool gas atoms absorb photons whose energy exactly matches the gap between energy levels, causing electrons to jump up to higher levels.

Why absorption lines match emission lines

The absorbed wavelengths are the same as the emitted ones because the same energy gaps are involved. When the excited electrons eventually de-excite, they emit photons in all random directions, not in the original direction of the white light. So the original beam has those wavelengths removed.

Key distinction: Emission = bright lines on dark background (hot gas). Absorption = dark lines on continuous spectrum (white light through cool gas). Same wavelengths, opposite appearance.
12.2.2.13

Energy Levels & Photon Transitions

When an electron transitions between energy levels E₁ and E₂:

hf = E₁ − E₂ (E₁ > E₂) Equivalently: hc/λ = E₁ − E₂

Reading energy level diagrams

Energy levels are drawn as horizontal lines. Values are negative (because energy must be added to free the electron). The ground state is the lowest (most negative), and 0 eV means the electron is free.

Example: Hydrogen energy levels

Typical values: −13.6 eV (ground), −3.4 eV, −1.5 eV, −0.85 eV, −0.54 eV

For Q19 type:
  • Longest wavelength = smallest energy difference = transition between two closest levels (e.g., −0.54 eV → −0.85 eV, ΔE = 0.31 eV)
  • Shortest wavelength = largest energy difference = transition from highest to lowest level (e.g., −0.54 eV → −3.4 eV, ΔE = 2.86 eV)

Visible spectrum photons

Visible light ranges from about 1.83 eV (red) to 3.66 eV (violet). To find visible transitions, calculate ALL possible energy differences and keep only those in the 1.83–3.66 eV range.

Worked example: Transitions from levels −0.54, −0.85, −1.5, −3.4 eV

All possible downward transitions to −3.4 eV:

  • −1.50 → −3.4: ΔE = 1.9 eV ✓ (visible)
  • −0.85 → −3.4: ΔE = 2.55 eV ✓ (visible)
  • −0.54 → −3.4: ΔE = 2.86 eV ✓ (visible)

Other transitions: −0.54→−1.5 = 0.96 eV (infrared), −0.85→−1.5 = 0.65 eV (infrared), −0.54→−0.85 = 0.31 eV (infrared). These are NOT visible.

Hydrogen energy levels and En = E₁/n²

For hydrogen, E₃ = E₁/9. So if the electron is in E₃ and falls to E₁ (ground state), compared to E₃ the ground state energy is: E₁ = 9 × E₃ (since E₁/E₃ = n₃²/n₁² = 9/1). So E₁ = 9E₃.

12.2.2.14

Bohr's Postulates & Atomic Stability

The classical problem

An orbiting electron is accelerating (changing direction). According to classical electromagnetism, an accelerating charge should continuously radiate energy, spiral inward, and crash into the nucleus. But atoms are stable! Classical physics couldn't explain this.

Bohr's postulates

  1. First postulate: Electrons can only exist in certain stable orbits with quantised angular momentum: L = mvr = nh/(2π), where n = 1, 2, 3, ... These orbits do NOT result in energy radiation.
  2. Second postulate: When an electron transitions from one orbit to another, it emits or absorbs a photon with energy equal to the difference: hf = E₁ − E₂
Key exam point: The best answer describing atomic stability is: "Electrons can only exist in certain stable orbits with quantised angular momentum, and these orbits do not result in energy loss." This matches option D in Q2 of the spec.

Note: Option A mentions de Broglie wavelengths — Bohr's original postulate was about angular momentum, but the connection to standing waves (integer wavelengths fitting the orbit circumference) provides an alternative justification. However, the core of Bohr's model is quantised angular momentum and no radiation in stable orbits.

α-scattering, radioactive decay, nuclear energy, binding energy, half-life

12.2.3.1

α-Particle Scattering & The Nucleus

Rutherford, Geiger & Marsden's experiment (1911)

A beam of α-particles was fired at thin gold foil. Observations:

  1. Most α-particles passed straight through → The atom is mostly empty space
  2. A small number were deflected at small angles → There is a concentration of positive charge
  3. Very few (~1 in 8000–20000) were deflected > 90° (backscattered) → The positive charge and most of the mass is concentrated in a tiny, dense nucleus

Conclusions

  • The atom is mostly empty space (most particles pass through)
  • The nucleus is very small compared to the atom (~10⁻¹⁵ m vs ~10⁻¹⁰ m)
  • The nucleus is positively charged (repels α-particles)
  • The nucleus contains most of the atom's mass
Q7 type: The α-scattering experiment provides information about the structure of the nucleus — specifically its small size and positive charge. (Not binding energy, not electron energy levels.)

Why faster α-particles get closer to the nucleus

Faster α-particles have more kinetic energy. As they approach the nucleus, their KE converts to electrical potential energy (due to electrostatic repulsion). Faster particles have more KE to "spend" against the repulsive force, so they can get closer before being turned back.

Trajectories of α-particles

  • Direct head-on: bounces straight back (180°)
  • Close pass: deflected through a large angle — curved path
  • Distant pass: very slight deflection or none

Conservation Laws & Nuclear Equations

Three quantities conserved in nuclear processes

  1. Nucleon number (A) — total number of protons + neutrons
  2. Proton number (Z) — also means charge is conserved
  3. Mass-energy — total mass + energy is constant (E = mc²)

Writing nuclear equations

Format: ᴬ_Z X where A = nucleon number (top), Z = proton number (bottom)

α decay: ²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Th + ⁴₂He A: 238 = 234 + 4 ✓ Z: 92 = 90 + 2 ✓ β⁻ decay: ¹⁴₆C → ¹⁴₇N + ⁰₋₁e + ν̄ A: 14 = 14 + 0 ✓ Z: 6 = 7 + (−1) ✓ β⁺ decay: ¹₁p → ¹₀n + ⁰₊₁e + ν A: 1 = 1 + 0 ✓ Z: 1 = 0 + 1 ✓

Nucleon numbers: 235 + 1 = 95 + 139 + X×1 + 7×0

236 = 234 + X → X = 2

Check proton numbers: 92 + 0 = 42 + 57 + 0 + 7×(−1) = 99 − 7 = 92 ✓

12.2.3.11

β Decay Using the Quark Model

β⁻ decay (neutron → proton)

Neutron (udd) → Proton (uud) + electron + antineutrino A down quark changes into an up quark: d → u + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ

This is mediated by the weak nuclear force (via a W⁻ boson).

β⁺ decay (proton → neutron)

Proton (uud) → Neutron (udd) + positron + neutrino An up quark changes into a down quark: u → d + e⁺ + νₑ
Q10 type trap: A neutron decays into a proton by emitting an electron and an antineutrino (not a neutrino, not a positron), with quark composition changing from udd to uud. This matches option A in the spec: "emitting a positron and a neutrino" is WRONG for β⁻. Watch the wording carefully! The correct answer for neutron decay is: electron + antineutrino, udd → uud.
Q15 type: When β⁻ particles are emitted with a range of energies, antineutrinos must also be emitted (to conserve energy and momentum). For β⁺ decay, neutrinos are emitted.
12.2.3.8

Spontaneous & Random Nature of Decay

Spontaneous means:

  • The decay is not affected by external conditions (temperature, pressure, chemical reactions)
  • The decay of one nucleus is not affected by the presence of other nuclei
  • There is no way to trigger or prevent it

Random means:

  • It is impossible to predict which specific nucleus will decay next, or when
  • Each nucleus has the same probability of decaying per unit time
  • A nucleus does not "age" — a nucleus that hasn't decayed in 1000 years is no more likely to decay in the next second than a freshly formed one
Evidence for randomness: Fluctuations in count rate measured by a Geiger counter. The clicks are irregular and unpredictable, even though the average rate is stable.
12.2.3.12 / 12.2.3.13

Mass-Energy, Mass Defect & Binding Energy

Einstein's mass-energy equivalence

E = mc² or for changes: ΔE = Δm × c²

Mass defect (Δm)

The mass of a nucleus is always less than the total mass of its separate individual nucleons. This "missing mass" is the mass defect.

Δm = (Z × mₚ + N × mₙ) − m_nucleus where Z = proton number, N = neutron number, mₚ = proton mass, mₙ = neutron mass

The "lost" mass has been converted into binding energy — the energy that holds the nucleus together.

Binding energy

The binding energy is the minimum energy needed to completely separate a nucleus into individual protons and neutrons. It equals the energy equivalent of the mass defect:

Binding Energy = Δm × c²

Binding energy per nucleon

To compare stability of different nuclei, we use binding energy per nucleon = BE / A

  • Higher BE per nucleon = more stable
  • Iron-56 has the highest BE per nucleon (~8.8 MeV) — most stable nucleus
  • Light nuclei (H, He) have lower BE per nucleon
  • Very heavy nuclei (U) also have lower BE per nucleon than iron

Converting atomic mass units to energy

1 u = 1.661 × 10⁻²⁷ kg Energy equivalent of 1 u: E = 1.661×10⁻²⁷ × (3.00×10⁸)² = 1.494×10⁻¹⁰ J In MeV: 1.494×10⁻¹⁰ / 1.60×10⁻¹³ ≈ 934 MeV So: 1 u ≈ 934 MeV (accept 931–934 MeV)
Worked example: Q17c — D-T fusion energy calculation

(i) Energy equivalent of 1 u:

E = mc² = 1.66×10⁻²⁷ × (3.00×10⁸)² = 1.494×10⁻¹⁰ J

Convert to MeV: 1.494×10⁻¹⁰ / 1.60×10⁻¹³ = 934 MeV

(ii) Energy released in D-T reaction:

²₁H + ³₁H → ⁴₂He + ¹₀n

Mass before = 2.01356 + 3.01551 = 5.02907 u

Mass after = 4.00151 + 1.00867 = 5.01018 u

Δm = 5.02907 − 5.01018 = 0.01889 u

Energy = 0.01889 × 934 = 17.6 MeV

12.2.3.15

Nuclear Fusion & Fission via Binding Energy

Nuclear fusion

Two light nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus. The products have higher binding energy per nucleon → energy is released.

Example: ²₁H + ¹₁H → ³₂He + energy Light nuclei (left of Fe on BE curve) move RIGHT → higher BE per nucleon → energy released

This is how the Sun works. It requires extremely high temperatures (~10⁷ K) because the nuclei must overcome their electrostatic repulsion (both positive). At such temperatures, nuclei have enough kinetic energy to get close enough for the strong nuclear force to take over.

Nuclear fission

A heavy nucleus splits into two lighter fragments. The products have higher binding energy per nucleon → energy is released.

Example: ²³⁵₉₂U + ¹₀n → fragments + neutrons + energy Heavy nuclei (right of Fe on BE curve) move LEFT → higher BE per nucleon → energy released
Both fusion and fission release energy because the products are more tightly bound (higher BE per nucleon) than the reactants. The "hill" of the BE per nucleon curve peaks at iron-56 — fusion works for nuclei lighter than iron, fission works for nuclei heavier than iron.

Why high temperature is needed for fusion

Both nuclei are positively charged → strong electrostatic repulsion. At very high temperatures, the nuclei have enough kinetic energy to overcome this repulsion and get close enough (~10⁻¹⁵ m) for the short-range strong nuclear force to bind them.

Activity, Decay Constant & Half-Life

Decay constant (λ)

The probability that an individual nucleus will decay per unit time. Units: s⁻¹ (or min⁻¹, year⁻¹).

Activity (A)

The rate of decay — number of disintegrations per second. Measured in becquerels (Bq) where 1 Bq = 1 decay/s.

A = λN A = activity (Bq) λ = decay constant (s⁻¹) N = number of undecayed nuclei

Half-life (t₁/₂)

The mean time taken for half of the active nuclei in a sample to decay. Also: the time for the activity to halve.

λ × t₁/₂ = ln 2 ≈ 0.693 t₁/₂ = 0.693 / λ λ = 0.693 / t₁/₂

Exponential decay equations

N = N₀ × e^(−λt) (undecayed nuclei) A = A₀ × e^(−λt) (activity) R = R₀ × e^(−λt) (count rate)

Finding N from mass

To find the number of nuclei from a given mass:

N = (mass / molar mass) × Nₐ where Nₐ = 6.02 × 10²³ mol⁻¹ (Avogadro's number)
Worked example: Q21 — Half-life of Americium-241

Given: mass = 5.1 μg = 5.1×10⁻⁶ g, A = 5.9×10⁵ Bq, molar mass = 241 g/mol

Step 1: Find N

N = (5.1×10⁻⁶ / 241) × 6.02×10²³ = 1.274×10¹⁶

Step 2: Find λ

A = λN → λ = A/N = 5.9×10⁵ / 1.274×10¹⁶ = 4.63×10⁻¹¹ s⁻¹

Step 3: Find t₁/₂

t₁/₂ = 0.693 / 4.63×10⁻¹¹ = 1.497×10¹⁰ s

Convert to years: 1.497×10¹⁰ / (365.25 × 24 × 3600) = ~474 years

Worked example: Q22b — Decay constant of Strontium-90

Given: t₁/₂ = 28.0 years

Convert to seconds: 28.0 × 365.25 × 24 × 3600 = 8.836×10⁸ s

λ = 0.693 / 8.836×10⁸ = 7.84×10⁻¹⁰ s⁻¹

Half-life definition: "The time required for the number of undecayed nuclei (or the activity) to reduce to half its initial value." Both parts (what reduces, by how much) should be stated for full marks.
Decay constant definition: "The probability of decay of a nucleus per unit time." Both "probability" and "per unit time" are needed.

Common Calculation Methods

1. Photoelectric calculations

Threshold wavelength: λ₀ = hc/Φ Max KE: Eₖ = hf − Φ = hc/λ − Φ Max speed: v = √(2Eₖ/m)

2. de Broglie

λ = h/(mv) If accelerated from rest through V: eV = ½mv² → v = √(2eV/m) → λ = h/√(2meV)

3. Energy level transitions

ΔE = E_upper − E_lower (always positive) f = ΔE / h λ = hc / ΔE

4. Binding energy

Δm = (Z×mₚ + N×mₙ) − m_nucleus BE = Δm × c² BE per nucleon = BE / A

5. Radioactive decay

λ = 0.693 / t₁/₂ A = λN N = N₀ e^(−λt) N (from mass) = (mass/molar_mass) × Nₐ
